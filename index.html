Stuck? Here's what I'd tell you if we hopped on a call.

By Tolani Omitokun Community Mentor April 22, 2026

Had a mentor call today with a builder who felt completely clueless. He'd shipped his MVP already, looked at it, and realized: this isn't actually solving anything. He could have quit. Instead we spent 20 minutes re-framing, and by the end he said "suddenly I don't feel clueless again." Writing this down so the next 10 people who hit the same wall can get there faster.

First thing: you're not alone

Every single builder in this hackathon is going to hit the wall where they look at their project and go, "wait, is this actually solving anything?" That wall is part of the process. If you're there right now, you're exactly where you're supposed to be.

Second thing: you probably don't need a new idea. You need a sharper problem.

Before you pivot, rebuild, or panic, answer this one question.

What problem are you solving that someone can't already solve by opening Claude and typing their question?

If the honest answer is "nothing," you don't need to throw your project away. You need to go deeper on the problem.

"A fitness app" is not a problem worth solving. "I'm a new mom with 20 minutes between nap cycles, I haven't moved my body in 18 months, and every fitness app assumes I have 45 minutes and a full gym" is a problem worth solving. Same category. Totally different build.

Build from what only YOU know

The winners of the last Opus hackathon were not the people with the cleverest tech. They were the people who built for a problem they actually live inside.

CrossBeam tackled California's ADU permit crisis. 429,000+ permits issued since 2018, 90%+ sent back for corrections, each cycle costing weeks and thousands of dollars. Mike's build lets you upload your corrections letter and construction plans. It reads blueprints with vision, interprets every correction with code citations, cross-references state and city law via live web research, and drafts a response package ready for an engineer to sign. Built on the Claude Agent SDK with 13 custom skills running concurrently.

TARA came out of Uganda. Kye built a transport appraisal system because Uganda spends over $1B a year on roads with only $20M on planning, and the process is still done manually by specialists. TARA analyzes dashcam footage frame-by-frame, segments the road, selects interventions with Uganda-calibrated costs, runs full cost-benefit analysis (NPV, EIRR, BCR, sensitivity, deterioration modeling), and has an equity module that identifies pedestrians without footpaths and school children on the carriageway. It shifted appraisal from pure economics to human-centered analysis.

The Bar

Neither of those is a chatbot. Both solve problems the builders live with. That's the bar.

So ask yourself: what do you know that nobody else in this Discord knows?

  • The actual workflow a friend or family member does by hand every week
  • The weird regional thing about your country or city that national platforms don't handle
  • The industry jargon, shortcut, or constraint that only insiders know
  • The job you used to do before this one
  • The thing that makes your family roll their eyes because "everyone just deals with it"

Domain expertise beats credentials. Start there.

Go deeper

Browse the last hackathon gallery to see the full cohort in context — not just the winners. Look at how people framed their problem, what they chose to demo, and where they got specific. That's the level to match.

Use Claude to think, not to pitch

Most people open Claude and ask, "give me a hackathon idea." That's how you get a generic idea. And a generic idea will not win this hackathon.

Treat Claude like a strategy partner, not a search engine. The clarity comes from the conversation, not the first reply.

Here's a prompt template you can copy into a fresh chat:

ROLE: You're my hackathon strategy partner, not an idea generator.
Challenge my framing before you answer.

CONTEXT:
- Built with Opus 4.7 Hackathon (April 21 to 28, 2026)
- Judging: Impact 30%, Opus 4.7 Use 25%, Demo 25%, Depth & Execution 20%
- Rules: open source, new work only, max team of 2
- Main model must be Opus 4.7

ABOUT ME:
- Background: [your career, skills, industry]
- Location and community: [where you live, who's around you]
- Lived experience: [what you've personally struggled with]

PROBLEMS I SEE (unfiltered, don't polish):
1. [Small daily problem from your life]
2. [Problem your family runs into]
3. [Workflow you've watched someone struggle with]
4. [Community-level problem]
5. [Industry-specific problem only insiders know]

OPUS 4.7 CAPABILITIES I WANT TO LEAN ON:
[Pick 2-3: 1M context window, self-verification, high-resolution vision,
xhigh effort, task budgets, file-system memory, Managed Agents, Agent SDK, Skills]

TASKS:
1. Which problem has the strongest Impact potential and why?
2. Which problem is most uniquely suited to Opus 4.7 (something that
   wouldn't have been possible with older models) and why?
3. Which one could ONLY I build, given my background?
4. If 1, 2, and 3 point to different problems, help me find the overlap
   or pick the strongest single bet.

Do not give me a polished idea. Ask me clarifying questions first.

Now iterate. Push back on the first answer. "What if I merged 3 and 5?" "What would this look like if only I could build it?" "What's the version of this that wasn't possible with a 2024 model?"

The goal is a sharpened problem, not a pitch deck.

What the judges actually reward

Read the rubric like it's a prompt. It tells you what to build for.

Criteria Weight What it's really asking
Impact 30% Is there a real, identifiable person whose life gets better?
Opus 4.7 Use 25% Did you do something that wouldn't have been possible with the older model?
Demo 25% Does it hit in 3 minutes? Is it cool to watch, not read?
Depth & Execution 20% Does it feel crafted, not hacked together the night before?

Translate that into your build priorities:

  • Impact: name the specific person you're building for. Not "users." One actual person.
  • Opus 4.7 Use: lean into what's new. 1M context window. Self-verification. High-resolution vision. xhigh effort level. Task budgets. File-system memory. Claude Managed Agents if you got in. If none of those are load-bearing for your project, you're leaving points on the table.
  • Demo: the best projects here are easier to demo than to explain. If your opener is "let me explain what I built," rework it. The opener should be "watch what happens when I..."
  • Depth: your first idea is not your final idea. Push past it.

If you score high on three and zero on one, you lose. Balance.

The demo is storytelling, not a feature tour

Three minutes is nothing. You can't walk through everything, so don't. The judges don't care about your tech stack. They care about whether they feel something when they watch.

The 3-minute arc that works

  1. 0:00 — 0:20 The person. Open with who. Not "introducing our AI-powered platform." Try: "My cousin spends every Sunday doing X by hand. Here's what that looks like." One specific human. One specific pain.
  2. 0:20 — 0:50 The pain. Show the before. A screen recording of the painful process. A screenshot of a 40-tab spreadsheet. A clip of someone sighing. Make them feel the weight.
  3. 0:50 — 2:20 The magic. Now show your tool. But not every feature. Pick the one moment that would surprise the judges. The "wait, it did THAT?" moment. Let it breathe. Don't rush the reveal.
  4. 2:20 — 2:50 Why Opus 4.7. Call out the capability that made this possible. "This blueprint analysis ran in 90 seconds because of Opus 4.7's high-resolution vision and 1M context window." Specific. Concrete.
  5. 2:50 — 3:00 The vision. One sentence. What does the world look like if this wins?

Tools that won't eat your day

  • DescriptText-based video editing. Auto-removes "ums." Great for narrated walkthroughs.
  • CapCutFree, fast, works on mobile or desktop, huge template library.
  • LoomFastest for record and trim. Good for scrappy demos.
  • ScreenStudio(Mac) Beautiful auto-zoom on clicks. Looks polished with zero effort.
  • OBSFull control if you already know your way around it.
  • QuickTime + iMovieWorks fine on Mac with zero installs.
Two Quick Tips

Record your voiceover separately from your screen capture. Your nerves will show up in a single take. Read the script, record each section, stitch it together. And watch your own demo muted. If it's not visually compelling with no sound, rewrite the script or rework the screen flow.

The 4 hard rules (break any, you're out)

  1. Open source. Backend, frontend, models, everything in the demo. Approved OSS license.
  2. New work only. Started from scratch during the hackathon. No previous repos.
  3. Team size max 2.
  4. No banned content. Nothing illegal, unethical, or using assets you don't have rights to.

Re-read these before you submit.

Quick technical guardrails

  • Main model must be Opus 4.7. You can use Sonnet 4.6 or Haiku 4.5 for sub-tasks where they make sense, but the hero model is 4.7.
  • API credits burn fast on xhigh. Use it for the hard reasoning steps, not for every call.
  • Stuck on Claude Code auth? If you keep hitting OAuth 400 when logging in or out, just set ANTHROPIC_API_KEY directly as an env var. It's a supported auth method and takes precedence over OAuth. Don't lose a day to auth.
  • Submissions: a 3-minute demo video, the open-source repo link, and a 100 to 200-word written summary.

The 30-minute unblock exercise

If you're still stuck after reading all of the above:

  • 10 minutes: write down 5 problems from your life, your family, your community, or the last job you held. Don't filter. Include the small ones.
  • 10 minutes: for each, ask "can someone solve this by just opening Claude?" If yes, cross it off.
  • 10 minutes: of the ones left, pick the one only you would know how to build for. That's your project.
You don't need to be clever. You need to be specific.

When to pivot vs. when to refine

Most builders think "I don't have the right idea" when they actually have "I don't have the right framing."

Refine if: you have a real problem but your build is too generic. Go back to the specific person you're building for and strip out everything that isn't for them.

Pivot if: your problem is solvable without AI at all, your problem isn't actually yours (you picked it because it sounded smart), or you can't demo it in 3 minutes.

Most of you need to refine, not pivot.

Engage. Ask questions. Have fun.

This hackathon is more than a competition. It's a week with hundreds of the most motivated builders on the platform. Use it.

  • Ask questions in #questions. Even ones you think are dumb. Someone else has the same one.
  • Drop into #office-hours. The Claude Code team is literally in the room.
  • Share your build-in-progress. Post a screenshot. Tag someone for feedback. The builders who share earliest get the best feedback loops.
  • Help someone else. If you just solved a bug that stumped you for 2 hours, post the fix. Karma is real in this Discord.
  • Pet photos welcome. Jason said so. Take it seriously.

The people you meet this week end up on your GitHub stars list, in your DMs when you're job hunting, and shipping with you on the next hackathon. Friends > prizes.

And have fun. If you're miserable, you're not going to ship your best work. Step away from the laptop. Go for a walk. Sleep. Come back with a fresh brain.

If you're feeling clueless right now, good. That's the feeling right before you lock in. The builders who end up on the podium felt exactly what you're feeling 36 hours before they shipped.

Go back to what only you would know how to build. Then build that.
